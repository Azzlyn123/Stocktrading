What you built is basically a simulator — good.

The key now is: is the simulator honest? Because a “too-perfect” simulator will create a bot that dominates backtests and dies live.

The top 8 things that make bots fake-good
	1.	Lookahead bias
Using data your bot wouldn’t have at that moment (even accidentally). Example: using the full candle’s high/low before the candle closes.
	2.	Survivorship bias
Testing only on today’s winners (like current S&P list). Real testing needs the universe as it existed then.
	3.	Bad fills / no slippage
If you assume you always get mid-price fills, your results are fantasy. You need spread + slippage + partial fills.
	4.	Ignoring latency
If your entry triggers at 10:05:00 but you “fill” at that exact price, that’s not real. Even a small delay changes outcomes.
	5.	Over-trading small edge
A tiny “edge” disappears once you add costs. You need to model fees + slippage + spread.
	6.	Training on the test set
If it “learns” from days you later use to evaluate it, it’ll look like it’s improving—because it’s memorizing.
	7.	Changing rules while measuring
If you tweak while evaluating, you can’t tell if it’s skill or curve-fit.
	8.	Not tracking “no trade” examples
If it only learns from taken trades, it becomes biased and doesn’t learn what it should’ve skipped.

If you want a bot that truly gets better, do this structure

Think of it like this:

Layer 1 (non-negotiable): Hard risk rules
	•	max loss per day
	•	fixed risk per trade (your tier system)
	•	max position size
	•	kill-switch

Layer 2: Strategy generates candidates

Your breakout + retest rules spit out possible trades.

Layer 3: AI only ranks candidates

The AI’s first job should be one of these:
	•	“Probability of reaching +1R before stop”
	•	“Expected R”
	•	“Quality score A/B/C”

Do NOT let AI control risk sizing or remove stops early on. That’s how accounts get nuked.

The “world-class” part comes from evaluation discipline

If you want elite-level progress, you need elite-level testing:

Use walk-forward testing (this matters a lot)

Example:
	•	Train on Days 1–60
	•	Test on Days 61–70 (no learning during this window)
	•	Roll forward: Train 1–70, test 71–80
This proves it can generalize.

Track the right scoreboard

Win rate is not enough. Track:
	•	expectancy (avg R per trade)
	•	profit factor
	•	max drawdown
	•	average slippage per trade
	•	performance by regime (trending vs choppy days, high ATR vs low ATR)
	•	time-of-day performance

The honest truth about “best in the world”

The best systems usually win because of:
	•	insane execution quality (fills, routing, latency)
	•	massive data + clean labeling
	•	tight risk controls
	•	constant monitoring for regime shifts

You can build a very strong system, but your edge will come from being more honest in testing than everyone else, not from the fanciest AI.
